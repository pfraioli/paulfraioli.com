Profile

Paul Fraioli is a Washington, DC-based expert on the geopolitics of technology, geo-economics, nuclear weapons and US foreign policy. He is Senior Fellow for Technology, Geopolitics and Strategy at the IISS, the world-leading think tank headquartered in London. As editor of Strategic Comments, he has wide-ranging expertise on global security and defense issues.

His work focuses on how technological innovation is reshaping global power, changing international economic and security relationships, and creating major strategic choices for governments, companies and defence institutions.

Featured publications

Strange Days: AI and the Next Industrial Revolution HTML PDF

A combinatorial revolution is under way in advanced technologies, driven by the simultaneous maturation of batteries, solar energy, semiconductors, sensors, electric motors and artificial intelligence (AI). AI systems have advanced by a factor of 21 every two years from 2010 to 2026, and autonomous AI agents are now capable of sustained, multistep action. These converging technologies are enabling the development of humanoid robots, with China leveraging its dominant industrial scale and Elon Musk pursuing vertical integration across his AI, energy, space-launch and manufacturing firms. Commercial deployment of these robots will likely begin later in the 2020s and accelerate in the 2030s. The social and strategic implications are significant. Humanoid robots will enter societies already experiencing disruptions from various directions. Labour-market disruption may prove less consequential than the gradual erosion of human agency through incremental delegation to opaque machine systems, particularly in democracies already weakened by the second-order effects of social media.

The US pivot on regulating AI diffusion HTML PDF

Donald Trump's announcement of a one-year waiver on the export of Nvidia's H200 chips to China marked a sharp turn away from Joe Biden's "small yard, high fence" approach to AI controls. By treating advanced chips as bargaining instruments in a wider contest for market share and influence, Washington is testing how far it can diffuse AI hardware without losing strategic leverage.
